Computer Vision

Information-theoretic Visual Segmentation & Lexical Learning: my undergraduate thesis for Commonwealth College and computer science honors, being completed under Professor Learned-Miller in the computer vision lab. I am exploring visual structure as a compression problem and how to learn visual codes.

The essence of this project is information theory-driven unsupervised learning of structure. Specifically, this capstone will address the problem of visual segmentation: deriving a set of distinct objects and regions and their locations from images. Consider a child drawing in scenes from a coloring book. The world is such a book without any of the lines. This is the visual segmentation problem: to identify meaningful parts of the world from the chaos of visual impressions.

Character Alignment for Scene Text Understanding: the word spotting task is the problem of recognizing instances of known text in the real world, such as recognizing street signs or business posters. I worked with PhD student Jacqueline Feild on the character alignment and similarity-scoring portion of a scene text recognition system for this purpose. I implemented alignment via a new probabilistic image representation developed by Professor Learned-Miller, the distribution field. I am 2nd author on a paper submitted to CVPR 2012 about this system and its improvement of state-of-the-art performance on a widely-used data set.

F3DB: I was responsible for data collection for this in-progress data set for 3D pose estimation of faces (the successor to the FDDB standard in face detection). I designed, implemented and assessed a large scale facial pose annotation system on Mechanical Turk with the feedback of (now graduated) PhD student Vidit Jain and Professor Learned-Miller. This system included a statistical quality assurance check to automatically accept and reject annotations.

The F3DB annotation interface.

Psychology

Time Order Judgement Apparatus : a novel device for biophysics experiments concerning time order judgements–the perceived order of observed events. I designed, assembled, and programmed the apparatus after discussing the need for it with neuroscience and behavior & cognitive psychology PhD student Patrick Taylor. It is currently in use as a covariate in studies of the precedence effect in psychoacoustics and other cognitive psychology research in the NeuroCognition and Perception lab.

The TOJ apparatus prototype before it was placed in its final case.

Math Cheating Task : this application was developed to study self-deceptive cheating for social psychology PhD student Mindi Rock. The app presents subjects with a series of arithmetic questions and the subject may cheat by pressing a button. This is explained as a bug leftover from testing.
